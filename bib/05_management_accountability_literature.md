# Algorithmic Accountability in Management/IS Literature

Literature survey conducted 2026-03-18. Focus: multi-agent AI accountability in organizational decision-making.

---

## Core Finding: Gap Confirmed

**No paper in top management/IS journals (MISQ, ISR, Management Science, Organization Science, AMJ, AMR) specifically theorizes accountability for multi-agent AI systems in organizational decision-making contexts.** The literature addresses single-AI accountability extensively but has not extended to multi-agent settings.

---

## Stream 1: AI as Organizational Agent (Agency Theory)

### Humberd & Latham (2026) - When AI Becomes an Agent of the Firm
- **Venue:** Journal of Management Studies
- **Argument:** Parallels between historical emergence of professional managers and current delegation to AI. As AI evolves toward autonomy, agency theory mechanisms need reconsideration.
- **Limitation:** Single AI-as-agent model, not multi-agent collectives.

### Jarrahi & Ritala (2025) - Rethinking AI Agents: A Principal-Agent Perspective
- **Venue:** California Management Review
- **Argument:** Applies principal-agent theory to AI governance. Identifies goal misalignment, information asymmetry, multi-agent complexity. Advocates "guided autonomy," transparency dashboards, orchestrator agents.
- **Note:** CLOSEST to our gap. Acknowledges multi-agent complexity as a challenge but remains conceptual, no formal treatment.

### Vanneste & Puranam (2024) - AI, Trust, and Perceptions of Agency
- **Venue:** Academy of Management Review
- **Argument:** How different levels of perceived AI agency affect human trust.
- **Limitation:** Does not address multi-agent settings.

---

## Stream 2: AI Governance Frameworks

### Papagiannidis, Mikalef & Conboy (2025) - Responsible AI Governance
- **Venue:** Journal of Strategic Information Systems
- **Argument:** Structural, relational, procedural practices for responsible AI governance. Positive impact on knowledge management and competitive performance.
- **Limitation:** Designed for single-AI deployments.

### Mikalef et al. (2025) - Responsible AI Starts with the Artifact
- **Venue:** EJIS (editorial)
- **Argument:** AI artifacts are agentic, autonomous, dynamic, inscrutable. Current principles-based approaches insufficient. Calls for artifact-centered responsible AI research.
- **Signal:** Editorial opening for agentic AI accountability work in top IS journals.

### Hillebrand, Raisch & Schad (2025) - Managing with AI: Integrative Framework
- **Venue:** Academy of Management Annals
- **Argument:** Reviews human-AI collaboration and algorithmic management. Introduces control-accountability alignment concept.
- **Limitation:** Not extended to multi-agent scenarios.

---

## Stream 3: Organizational Decision-Making and AI

### Shrestha, Ben-Menahem & von Krogh (2019) - Organizational Decision-Making in the Age of AI
- **Venue:** California Management Review
- **Argument:** Five contingency factors for human vs. AI decisions. Three structural configurations (full delegation, hybrid, aggregated).
- **Limitation:** Predates agentic AI era, does not consider multi-agent architectures.

### Anthony, Bechky & Fayard (2023) - "Collaborating" with AI
- **Venue:** Organization Science
- **Argument:** System view where technology is a counterpart, not a tool. AI as actor in multiagent, goal-oriented system.
- **Limitation:** Focuses on human-AI, not AI-AI coordination.

### Stelmaszak, Joshi & Constantiou (2025) - AI as an Organizing Capability
- **Venue:** Journal of Management Studies
- **Argument:** AI arises from relations among human and algorithmic actors as organizing capability (connectivity, codependence, emergence).
- **Relevance:** Relational ontology applicable to multi-agent accountability.

---

## Stream 4: Accountability Theory

### Zeiser (2024) - Owning Decisions: The Attributability-Gap
- **Venue:** Science and Engineering Ethics
- **Argument:** AI decision-support creates "attributability gap" by obscuring value-judgments. Distinguishes attributability, answerability, accountability.
- **Key insight:** This gap COMPOUNDS in multi-agent systems but compounding effect not theorized.

### Collina, Sayyadi & Provitera (2023) - AI Accountability
- **Venue:** California Management Review
- **Argument:** Three liability models (developer, user, shared). Shared accountability risks diluting individual responsibility.
- **Limitation:** Does not consider multi-agent architectures.

---

## Stream 5: Multi-Agent AI Risks and Governance (Technical)

### Hammond et al. (2025) - Multi-Agent Risks from Advanced AI
- **Venue:** arXiv:2502.14143 (Cooperative AI Foundation)
- **Argument:** Three failure modes (miscoordination, conflict, collusion) + seven risk factors. 50+ researchers from DeepMind, Anthropic, CMU, Harvard.
- **Limitation:** AI safety framing, not organizational governance.

### Syrnikov et al. (2026) - Institutional AI: Governing LLM Collusion
- **Venue:** arXiv:2601.11369
- **Argument:** "Institutional AI" = mechanism design in institution-space. Governance graph as public immutable manifest. Reduces collusion from 50% to 5.6%.
- **Relevance:** Pioneering bridge between institutional theory and multi-agent governance.

### Survey on Responsibility in LLM-based MAS (2025)
- **Venue:** ResearchGate preprint
- **Scope:** 60+ works. Four pillars: Reliability, Transparency, Accountability, Fairness. Maps coordination patterns to pillars.
- **Limitation:** CS perspective, lacks organizational theory framing.

---

## Stream 6: Agentic AI in Organizations (Emerging)

### Hughes, Dwivedi et al. (2025) - AI Agents: Multi-Expert Analysis
- **Venue:** Journal of Computer Information Systems
- **Argument:** AI agents reshape industries by decentralizing decision-making. Attribution and shared accountability identified as critical challenges.

### Chinnaraju (2026) - When AI Agents Act
- **Venue:** IJRSI
- **Argument:** "Artificial agency" = delegation of decision authority without legal personhood. Integrates agency theory + corporate governance + decision rights theory.

### Bommarito, Katz & Bommarito (2025) - Governing AI Agents in Law and Finance
- **Venue:** SSRN
- **Argument:** Three organizational models (centralized, federated, embedded) + RACI matrices for oversight. Five-layer regulatory stack.

### Pandey (2025) - Agentic AI Governance Framework
- **Venue:** SSRN
- **Argument:** Six-principled lifecycle model. Existing frameworks (NIST, ISO 42001) lack implementation depth for multi-agent systems.

---

## What's Missing (The Gap)

1. **No organizational theory of emergent accountability**: who is accountable when collective agent behavior produces outcomes no single agent intended?
2. **No empirical study** of accountability assignment across multi-agent AI deployments
3. **No bridging work** connecting technical MAS risk taxonomy (Hammond et al.) with organizational governance theory
4. **The attributability gap (Zeiser 2024) compounds in multi-agent systems** but this has not been theorized
5. **No formal accountability mechanism** (like Shapley/Myerson attribution) proposed in management literature for multi-agent AI systems
